Notes

Intro

Corporate Strategy refers to the strategy that multi-business corporations use to compete as a collection of multiple businesses. It is qualitatively different from strategy for a single-business or business strategy. (  [Puranam16]  )

Corporate Strategy being about multi-businesses, it becomes key to define what a business is. The usual accepted definition is that a business is characterized by its business model. So if a firm addresses different segments of customers with radically different value propositions or value architecture, then it is a multiple-business firm.

Now reality, as ever, can be rather blurry. A multi-national firm can be seen as comprising different businesses (e.g. multi-domestic firms) or on the contrary as offering worldwide a single value proposition (e.g. trading firm).

Likewise, when the launch of a new product requires tremendous investment (e.g. aircraft programme), it can be viewed as a business in its own right, although the business model remains unchanged.

Corporate advantage

For Corporate Strategy, a firm competes with other potential owners for businesses. There are two central questions:

  • is the firm the best owner for a given business (i.e. would worth more to someone else),

  • how can you obtain “corporate advantage” i.e.yield more value by combining several businesses

A firm has Corporate advantage if owning together the two businesses A and B, yields more value than the sum of the value of the businesses owned separately - that is V[AB] > V(A) + V(B).

Businesses can be owned jointly or operated jointly

Two types of players are involved in Corporate Strategy : financial investors (assemble portfolio i.e. acquire cash flow rights over returns but with limited decision rights) and strategic investors (operate and modify the acquired businesses).

When Value is measured as an NPV (investment logic), there are two ways to improve the NPV: by increasing future cash flows or by decreasing the dicount rate.

You can create corporate advantage through selection of a good business portfolio (risk diversification)

Creating Value through diversification

[unrelated businesses, same risk profile => same expected return with lower risk profile = less extreme payments]

3 Companies (500, 1⁄2) - (0, 1⁄2) -> 1⁄3 (1500, 1⁄6), (1000, 1⁄3), (500, 1⁄3) , (0, 1⁄6) -> same average (expected value), less extreme payments -> lower the risk of ‘0’ -> decrease the discount rate

Why would a financial investor acquire a company:

  • Buy an under-evaluated company and resell it at its true value,

  • Buy a company and sell it in parts, (when the sum of the part is less than the parts),

  • Buy a company, restructure it and resell

Synergies

While a financial investor can only impact the discount rates by reducing and diversifying risks, a strategic investor can change the cash flows (e.g. by increasing revenues or decreasing costs) or their timing.

Operational synergy means that two businesses operated jointly (decisions are coordinated) are more valuable than operated independently. This can sometimes be achieved through strategic partnerships, but usually requires common ownership.

Other reasons (non-operational synergies): - market access - resource access (talent, financing, raw material, IP, …) - Tax optimization

Operational synergies can be characterized according to two dimensions: the level of similarity of the underlying resources (are we linking similar or dissimilar resources when two businesses are jointly operated) and the extend of required modification.

This may seem trivial but assessing the right level can be extremely challenging (particularly for a business that is insufficiently know / understood ).

ADD GRAPHIC

Four quadrants: consolidation, combination, Connection, Customization

If resources are very similar (at the extreme identical - case of horizontal merger), then economies of scale might be experienced which will translate into higher performance and value creation.

separatly If resources are somehow related, while not strictly identical, economies of scope are likely to be experience, which again can translate into value correlations

If resources are very distinct (e.g. conglomerate merger) then it might become more challenging to generate higher performance by operating the two businesses together.

Complex value chains can exhibit partial similarity (e.g. similar retail networks) while being different at the same time (dissimilar R&D).

The extent of modification represents the amount of post-merger change (frictions) that is required to operate jointly the two businesses (e.g. suppress duplicate, integrate processes or information systems). The more change is necessary, the more it is likely inhibit the value created by the synergies.

Combinaison
The two firms have similar value chains and rely on rather identical resources. Post-merger restructuration will be limited. The two business will continue to be operated in parallel.

The main objective is to develop horizontally and grow production volume (or limit competition).

  • If the industry is subject to economies of scale, production costs will decrease with volume

  • volume will grow the bargaining power with suppliers and translate into higher purchasing discounts

  • when the size becomes significant, incumbent players may compete less out of fear of retaliation. This usually will raise customer price.

Regulators are vigilant and may block such combination on anti-trust ground.

Consolidation
The two firms have similar value chains and rely on rather identical resources. However existing redundancies must be eliminated through restructuring. Synergies are mainly created by rationalization of duplicates and elimination of capital employed .

  • Existing non-productive activities (e.g. HQ, IT, Procurement) are merged and simplified (headcount reduction, site closing)

  • Mutualization is promoted (e.g. shared service, reduction of warehouses and plants if feasible)

  • Strategic decision-making is centralised as well as corporate finance (e.g. treasury)

As a result, the level of operating costs (mainly indirect) as well as the level of capital employed are reduced. Mechanically the ROCE improves.
